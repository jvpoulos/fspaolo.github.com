Wealth, Officeholding, and Slaveholder Ideology in Antebellum Georgia

[paper] [online appendix] [pre-analysis plan]

Does personal wealth determine the ideology of officeholders? This paper investigates the role of personal wealth in politics using a natural experiment in public land redistribution. In 1805, the state of Georgia conducted the first land lottery in U.S. history. The vast majority of eligible adult white males participated and about one-in-seven participants won a land prize worth over half of median annual income. Using the lottery as a natural experiment, I find that randomly-assigned wealth significantly reduces legislators' support of policies favored by the slaveholder class. This result demonstrates that personal wealth influences policymaking, but not in the direction anticipated by elite theories of American politics. I find lottery wealth has no effect on selection into office or the length of tenure in office.
